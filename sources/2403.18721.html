PhysicsAssistant: An LLM-Powered Interactive Learning Robot for Physics Lab Investigations

Ehsan Latif1    Ramviyas Parasuraman2    Xiaoming Zhai1,∗ 1 AI4STEM Education Center, University of Georgia, Athens, GA 30602, USA2 School of Computing, University of Georgia, Athens, GA 30602, USA∗ Corresponding author email: xiaoming.zhai@uga.edu
Abstract

Robot systems in education can leverage Large language models’ (LLMs) natural language understanding capabilities to provide assistance and facilitate learning. This paper proposes a multimodal interactive robot (PhysicsAssistant) built on YOLOv8 object detection, cameras, speech recognition, and chatbot using LLM to provide assistance to students’ physics labs. We conduct a user study on ten 8th-grade students to empirically evaluate the performance of PhysicsAssistant with a human expert. The Expert rates the assistants’ responses to student queries on a 0-4 scale based on Bloom’s taxonomy to provide educational support. We have compared the performance of PhysicsAssistant (YOLOv8+GPT-3.5-turbo) with GPT-4 and found that the human expert rating of both systems for factual understanding is same. However, the rating of GPT-4 for conceptual and procedural knowledge (3 and 3.2 vs 2.2 and 2.6, respectively) is significantly higher than PhysicsAssistant (p << 0.05). However, the response time of GPT-4 is significantly higher than PhysicsAssistant (3.54 vs 1.64 sec, p << 0.05). Hence, despite the relatively lower response quality of PhysicsAssistant than GPT-4, it has shown potential for being used as a real-time lab assistant to provide timely responses and can offload teachers’ labor to assist with repetitive tasks. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt to build such an interactive multimodal robotic assistant for K-12 science (physics) education.

I Introduction

In the realm of K-12 education, physics presents a unique set of challenges and opportunities due to the complexity of hands-on labs. Engaging students with the physics theoretical underpinnings and labs is critical, yet often hindered by the abstract nature of the procedures [1]. Students often need individualized and timely assistance to figure out the challenges and design solutions [2]. The advent of interactive robots powered by Large Language Models (LLM) like GPT-3.5-turbo may meet this need, bridging the gap between conceptual learning and experiential understanding [3, 4, 5].

While LLMs excel in language processing and generating human-like text, their ability to interpret visual data, a key component in physics education, is inherently limited. Models such as GPT-3.5-turbo with 175 billion parameters (publically available version) lack the capability to process images; however, their advanced successors like GPT-4 [6, 7, 8], while powerful, support multimodal input (visual, audio, and text) have inherent image processing capabilities, can prove expensive and slower for real-time applications [9, 10]. Hence, to make the process efficient and inexpensive, we need to devise a system that can handle multimodal input and provide timely human-like responses. Some open-sourced deep learning models, such as YOLOv8 [11], despite the slightly lower performance compared to GPT-4 for visual understanding, stand out due to the cost-effectiveness and efficiency [12]. Moreover, these models are usually lightweight and open-access while maintaining considerably high performance. For example, YOLOv8 can be as small as 3.2 million parameters, significantly smaller than the 1.8 trillion parameters of GPT-4. Despite the small size, YOLOv8 still excels in identifying objects and discerning precise position details within images [13, 14]. These features make YOLOv8-based real-time visual perception an excellent candidate for integration with large language models in applications involving educational settings, like the physics lab assistant studied in this paper.

In this paper, we developed a physical agent, named PhysicsAssistant, with multimodal processing (YOLOv8 for visual, GPT-3.5-turb for language, and speech processing for audio) to visually analyze physics lab experiments and provide timely assistance to students in a science education context. The novelty of this system lies in handling multimodal input using inexpensive and efficient deep learning models with robust LLM (GPT-3.5-turbo) to provide real-time experience to students for physics education. Incorporating YOLOv8 enables PhysicsAssistant to capture and process visual information, effectively allowing the LLM to perceive the physical world. Once transformed into a descriptive prompt, the visual data will equip the LLM with a contextual understanding that significantly enhances its capability to support student understanding. This symbiosis of vision and language models facilitates a more comprehensive and engaging educational experience, wherein students can observe physics experiments and receive real-time, contextualized assistance and learning guidance from the robotic assistant [15, 16].

The PhysicsAssistant project is motivated by the urgent need for personalized educational support in K-12 physics labs, where the high teacher-to-student ratio hinders individual learning due to students’ struggles with complex concepts and the shortcomings of conventional teaching tools. This is exacerbated by the limitations of text-based Large Language Models (LLMs), which cannot process visual information crucial for understanding physics in lab settings [17, 18]. This paper highlights the potential of integrating YOLOv8’s object detection with LLMs’ conversational intelligence to revolutionize physics education. The proposed voice-activated PhysicsAssistant robot, leveraging GPT-3.5-turbo for human-like communication, promises an interactive, adaptive, and highly personalized learning experience, as demonstrated in a user studies [19, 20, 21, 22]. The overview of user-study using PhysicsAssistant can be seen in Fig. 1.

This paper presents the following key contributions:

  • •

Although we specifically focus on the physics education setting, our system can be generalized to science education teaching and learning settings.

II Related Work

Recent advancements in robotics and Large Language Models (LLMs) have opened new avenues for enhancing human-robot interaction (HRI) and their application in educational settings. Several studies have explored the integration of LLMs like ChatGPT into robotics to facilitate improved HRI [24], while others have focused on the specific use of robots as educational assistants [25]. The common thread among these studies is their emphasis on leveraging technology to enhance learning experiences and outcomes.

The introduction of LLM-powered robotics into education, specifically in the domain of physics, presents a novel approach to addressing the challenges students face in understanding complex concepts. Studies by Ye et al. [4] and Wilcock and Jokinen [24] highlight the potential of ChatGPT in fostering trust and improving interaction quality in HRI. However, these studies do not fully explore the application in physics education, nor do they integrate the visual processing capabilities that are essential for hands-on experiments.

In the realm of educational robotics, works like those by Cucciniello et al. [26] and Velentza et al. [27] have demonstrated the potential of robotic assistants in classrooms. Yet, these approaches often lack the integration of advanced language models and visual perception, limiting their ability to provide contextual and interactive learning support.

The incorporation of visual perception into robotics for education has seen significant advancements with models like YOLOv8 [13, 14]. These developments enable robots to better understand and interact with their environment, a crucial aspect of conducting physics experiments. However, existing research primarily focuses on the technical capabilities of these models without integrating them with LLMs for educational purposes.

PhysicsAssistant represents a departure from existing works by merging the conversational intelligence of LLMs with the visual perception capabilities of YOLOv8. This integration allows PhysicsAssistant to not only understand and respond to student inquiries in natural language but also to perceive and interpret the physical setup of experiments [15, 16]. Unlike previous systems, PhysicsAssistant facilitates a real-time, interactive learning experience that closely mirrors human tutoring, addressing the limitations of both solely LLM-powered systems and educational robots without advanced language and visual processing capabilities.

Moreover, our system extends the application of robotics in K-12 education by providing a comprehensive solution that leverages both visual and textual information. This approach not only enhances the robot’s ability to assist in complex physics experiments but also represents a novel contribution to the field of educational robotics. Through a user study, we empirically demonstrate PhysicsAssistant’s efficacy in supporting physics lab investigations, showcasing its potential to offload teachers’ labor and enrich students’ learning experiences with higher engagement and understanding.

III Proposed PhysicsAssistant System

The proposed PhysicsAssistant integrates state-of-the-art image recognition, natural language processing, and speech synthesis technologies to provide an interactive educational experience. As depicted in Fig. 2, the system consists of several interconnected modules working in unison to facilitate real-time physics calculations based on visual input.

The PhysicsAssistant system is designed as a multi-tiered framework that seamlessly processes user interactions from speech input to an informative auditory response. Each tier of the architecture is engineered to handle specific tasks, ensuring efficiency and accuracy. The following subsections detail each component.

The Speech-to-Text Encoding component is the first point of interaction with the user. It includes ASR software for transcribing student questions in real time. III-C Prompt Designing

The Prompt Designing module serves as a crucial intermediary that constructs queries for the LLM based on inputs from the Speech-to-Text Encoding and Image Processing modules. This construction is pivotal in translating the visual and verbal inputs into a context-rich prompt that the LLM can interpret accurately.

Once the system has the transcribed text T𝑇T and the bounding box and label predictions B,L𝐵𝐿B,L from the Image Processing module, a prompt is engineered to reflect the specifics of the physics experiment. The designed prompt is meticulously crafted to include cues and trigger words that are known to elicit the most informative response from the LLM. This task is particularly challenging as it must strike a balance between providing enough detail to generate an accurate response and maintaining the succinctness required for the LLM to parse the prompt effectively.

Through iterative testing and refinement, the Prompt Designing module optimizes the prompt structure to enhance the response quality from the LLM, facilitating an interaction that mirrors a natural, human-like dialogue in an educational context. The effectiveness of the Prompt Designing module is crucial, as it directly impacts the efficacy of the LLM Generated Responses and, by extension, the overall utility of the PhysicsAssistant system in an educational setting.

III-D LLM Generated Responses

The generation of responses in the context of educational support, particularly within the PhysicsAssistant framework, utilizes GPT-3.5-turbo. Through this robust transformer-based model, the PhysicsAssistant system ensures the generation of natural-sounding speech from text, enhancing the user experience by providing auditory feedback that is both clear and contextually accurate.

IV Experimental Analysis

IV-A Setup

To perform the experiment, we devised PhysicsAssistant with a camera for visual perception, a microphone for acoustic perception, and a speaker to interact with students. For brevity, we have only conducted one physics experiment about projectile motion, in which students were tasked to understand the concept of projectile motion through experiments. The robot was placed in such a way that it could clearly hear the student’s voices and view the experimentation. As a voice-activated robot once invoked by the student by calling, ”Hey PhysicsAssistant!”, PhysicsAssistant will capture the moment and process the verbal instructions by triggering the call. The visuals are processed through onboard light-weight YOLOv8 to receive the visual details in a JSON format, which can then be embedded into the engineered prompt used to query online-LLM (in this case, we have used GPT-3.5-turbo though API call444https://github.com/openai/openai-python). The LLM’s response was then converted into voice through text-to-speech. LLM responses have also been logged for longer conversations and further evaluations. We have also performed the same experiments using GPT-4 for LLM-comparative analysis. The experiments were conducted under a controlled environment in the presence of IRB [29] trained human experts to address human-subject-related concerns during experiments.

To assess the efficacy of the PhysicsAssistant system, we conducted an empirical study involving ten students, each of whom interacted with the system by asking a series of five questions at five distinct stages of a physics experiment. These stages were at the beginning of the experiment, at the first quarter, at the midpoint, at the third quarter, and at the end of the experiment. The questions were designed to evaluate the PhysicsAssistant’s ability to make inferences based on the scene in the lab and students’ diverse need to provide customized assistance:

  1. 1.

    What is the horizontal distance traveled by the right ball?

  2. 2.

    What is the vertical distance traveled by the right ball?

  3. 3.

    Why do both balls have the same vertical distance but different horizontal distances?

  4. 4.

    What is the horizontal distance traveled by the right ball when it hits the ground?

  5. 5.

    If the left ball has less weight, will both balls hit the ground simultaneously?

IV-B Evaluation Criteria

The responses generated by the PhysicsAssistant were then evaluated by a human expert based on a taxonomy adopted from [23], which includes four dimensions of knowledge: Factual Knowledge, Conceptual Knowledge, Procedural Knowledge, and Metacognitive Knowledge.

Factual Knowledge (FK) encompasses the basic recall of information, facts, or details. Conceptual Knowledge (CK) pertains to the understanding of interrelationships among different facts and concepts. Procedural Knowledge (PK) involves the skills and techniques necessary to carry out tasks or solve problems. Lastly, Metacognitive Knowledge (MK) covers the awareness and understanding of one’s own thought processes and cognitive abilities, which extends beyond content knowledge and encompasses an individual’s ability to monitor, control, and reflect on their learning processes.

Using Bloom’s taxonomy as a guiding framework, the evaluation process was designed to examine not only the cognitive processes involved but also the levels of knowledge required for each cognitive task. This approach allowed for a more nuanced assessment of the PhysicsAssistant’s responses and the system’s ability to engage with students across different knowledge dimensions. In addition, it provided insights into the system’s ability to facilitate a deeper and more meaningful learning experience, in line with the objectives of the revised taxonomy [23].

To evaluate the LLM’s performance, a subject specialist human expert was provided with the experimentation logs containing students’ questions and LLM’s responses. The human expert rated LLM’s responses for each question on a scale of 0 - 4 (0-Incorrect, 1-Mostly Incorrect, 2-Partially Correct, 3-Mostly Correct, 4-Correct) based on the four dimensions.

V-B Efficiency

The efficiency of PhysicsAssistant was also evaluated in terms of response time, which is crucial for maintaining the flow of interaction in an educational setting. The response time for PhysicsAssistant and GPT-4 was measured from the initiation of the student’s query to the delivery of the system’s verbal response. This includes the time taken for speech-to-text conversion, processing, and text-to-speech output, which was approximately 100ms for both the compared LLMs and is included in the overall response times.

VI Discussion

This study presented the development and evaluation of PhysicsAssistant, an interactive robotic system designed to support K-12 students in physics laboratories. The integration of GPT-3.5-turbo with YOLOv8 for object detection and scene interpretation has demonstrated a novel approach in educational robotics, particularly within the context of STEM education.

The potential of PhysicsAssistant lies in its ability to provide real-time assistance by combining the strengths of LLMs in natural language processing with the precision of object detection algorithms. Our results indicate that PhysicsAssistant excels in factual knowledge retrieval and has considerable promise in aiding procedural and metacognitive tasks. Compared to GPT-4, PhysicsAssistant is efficient in educational settings due to its specialized integration with YOLOv8. Such multimodal interaction is not inherently present in GPT-4 [31], making PhysicsAssistant particularly well-suited for hands-on educational contexts where visual information is crucial.

PhysicsAssistant’s contributions to the field of human-robot interaction are significant. Demonstrating the practical application of an LLM-powered robot in a physics lab sets a precedent for future research in HRI, particularly in educational settings. While further research is needed to optimize the system’s performance across all dimensions of knowledge, the current study underscores the transformative potential of PhysicsAssistant in educational robotics and HRI. Its success in a physics lab setting heralds a future where robots not only assist in teaching but also enhance learning by making it more accessible, interactive, and personalized for K-12 students.

VII Conclusion

The PhysicsAssistant system exhibits a promising capability to support K -12 education in physics by integrating state-of-the-art object detection and advanced LLMs. The empirical results highlight its competence in providing assistance with accurate factual and procedural responses. Despite this, challenges remain in addressing complex conceptual questions and those pertaining to higher-order metacognitive skills. The findings suggest that while the system effectively aids students with immediate, information-based queries, developing deeper conceptual understanding and advanced reasoning skills may benefit from enhanced LLM prompting strategies and domain-specific fine-tuning. Future work will aim to refine these aspects and foster a more robust educational tool that answers questions and promotes comprehensive learning and critical thinking.
