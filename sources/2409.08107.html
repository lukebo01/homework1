Abstract

Integrating named entity recognition (NER) with automatic speech recognition (ASR) can significantly enhance transcription accuracy and informativeness. In this paper, we introduce WhisperNER, a novel model that allows joint speech transcription and entity recognition. WhisperNER supports open-type NER, enabling recognition of diverse and evolving entities at inference. Building on recent advancements in open NER research, we augment a large synthetic dataset with synthetic speech samples. This allows us to train WhisperNER on a large number of examples with diverse NER tags. During training, the model is prompted with NER labels and optimized to output the transcribed utterance along with the corresponding tagged entities. To evaluate WhisperNER, we generate synthetic speech for commonly used NER benchmarks and annotate existing ASR datasets with open NER tags. Our experiments demonstrate that WhisperNER outperforms natural baselines on both out-of-domain open type NER and supervised finetuning.

I Introduction

Recent advancements in both speech processing and natural language processing (NLP) have dramatically expanded the capabilities of automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems. These improvements, driven by large-scale models like Whisper [1], have significantly enhanced transcription accuracy. Meanwhile, the emergence of powerful NLP models, particularly large language models (LLMs), has led to major breakthroughs in tasks like language understanding, text generation, and named entity recognition (NER).

Traditionally, speech-driven applications have relied on pipeline architectures, where ASR systems transcribe speech into text, which is then processed by NLP models for tasks such as sentiment analysis, question answering, and NER. While this approach has proven effective across a range of applications, it suffers from error accumulation — where transcription errors in the ASR stage propagate through the pipeline, reducing the performance of downstream NLP tasks. This challenge is particularly evident in complex tasks requiring high accuracy, such as NER.

A growing area of interest is the integration of speech models into a variety of NLP tasks beyond transcription, allowing models to process spoken language directly and eliminate intermediate stages [2, 3, 4, 5]. For instance, spoken language understanding models have been developed to perform tasks like intent detection and NER [6, 2, 5] directly from speech, bypassing the need for separate ASR components. These end-to-end models have shown promising results by jointly optimizing for both speech and language understanding.

Recently, open NER [7, 8, 9] has gained significant attention for its ability to generalize to new and unseen entities, offering a flexible alternative to traditional NER systems that are restricted by fixed entity types. [7] introduce a targeted distillation method, where student models are trained through instruction tuning. [8, 9] suggested using a tailored, small size transformer encoders, designed specifically for the NER problem, and showed that this models can outperform LLM based NER models. [10] propose a method for improving information extraction results through annotation guidelines, and [11] explored the importance of “negative instances”, i.e, non-entity spans, for improving contextual information and label boundaries. However, integrating open type NER directly into ASR systems remains an unexplored area.

In this paper, we propose WhisperNER, a unified model that simultaneously performs speech transcription and named entity recognition, based on the Whisper ASR model [1]. By integrating NER directly into the ASR process, WhisperNER eliminates the need for separate ASR and NER components, thereby reducing the risk of error propagation. The model supports open type NER, enabling it to recognize and tag a wide range of entities during inference, offering a more accurate and flexible approach to entity recognition from speech.

To build WhisperNER, we leverage a large-scale synthetic dataset released from [9]. We augment a subset of the textual open NER dataset with corresponding synthetic audio samples. The final dataset contains 350350350K samples and ∼1.8similar-toabsent1.8\sim 1.8M unique entity types. Our approach draws motivation from recent approaches which incorporates prior information and context into the decoding process by prompting the model on domain information [12] or keyword phrases [13, 14]. We train WhisperNER by prompting the model with NER type, for the task of joint transcription and NER tagging (See Figure 1). At inference time, WhisperNER can generalize to novel entity types not observed during training.

This paper makes the following contributions: (i) Propose WhisperNER, a novel model for joint open NER and ASR; (ii) Introducing new speech and text NER and open-NER benchmarks (iii) Conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate the superiority of WhisperNER.

To encourage future research and reproducibility, we will make our source code, datasets, and models publicly available.

II Our Method

In this section we describe our approach, WhisperNER, which extends the Whisper [1] ASR model to allow for joint open NER and speech recognition. The model is designed to output a tagged transcription sequence given an audio input, effectively performing both tasks simultaneously.

II-B Negative Sampling and Entity Type Dropout

In our experiments, we find that including negative samples, i.e., prompting the model with entity labels not presented in the speech utterance, to significantly enhance the model’s generalization performance, in line with findings in previous literature on open-type NER [8]. We explored various negative sampling approaches and chose to use the complete entity set from a negative example, randomly selected from the training set, which proved most effective.

Additionally, we applied random entity type dropout during training, as inspired by Sainz et al. [10]. This involves randomly eliminating a subset of entity types from the prompt and output sequence, compelling the model to focus on the remaining defined entity types. We found this approach to reduce entity hallucination risks during inference – where the model output entities not presented in the input prompt. Also, we incorporate random shuffling of entities in the input prompt to further promote generalization across diverse input sequences. For a detailed analysis of these methodologies and their impacts, see Section III-D.

III Experiments

In this section, we evaluate WhisperNER against naturall, pipeline based baselines, using NER and open type NER speech datasets.

Datasets.  Our evaluation setup consists of open type NER benchmarks and commonly used NER benchmarks. For the open type NER benchmarks we utilize three frequently used speech datasets with diverse linguistic and contextual properties. These datasets include VoxPopuli [15], LibriSpeech [16], and Fleurs [17], each offering unique challenges and opportunities for evaluating NER models, particularly in open-type NER contexts. For the multilingual datasets, we focus specifically on the English subset of the data. As this ASR datasets do not include NER labels, we adopt an LLM based annotation approach, in line with the method described in [9]. We utilize the prompts derived approach to extract open type NER from the raw transcriptions of each dataset. See [9] for full details of the approach and the specific prompt. This approach results with a wide and diverse set of NER annotations, allowing us to test the capabilities of our approach to identify a broader range of entity types that are not confined to traditional categories; Thus, enhancing our understanding of the model’s generalization in real-world applications.

This annotation scheme results with the following ASR-NER datasets: (i) VoxPopuli-NER: This dataset comprises 879879879 samples. It is annotated with 246924692469 unique entity types, providing a comprehensive basis for political and geographical entity recognition ; (ii) LibriSpeech-NER: Based on the LibriSpeech-clean dataset, with 160416041604 samples derived from audio book readings, includes annotations for 367436743674 unique entity types. This dataset is particularly valuable for testing the model’s ability to identify personal names and locations within narrative contexts; (iii) Fleurs-NER: Consists of 441441441 samples, annotated for 144014401440 unique entity types.

In addition, we utilize commonly used, textual NER benchmarks [8, 9, 10, 11], namely MIT-Movie [18] and MIT-Restaurant [18]. These datasets consists of ∼14similar-toabsent14\sim 14K and ∼10similar-toabsent10\sim 10K, with 121212 and 888 entity types, respectively. To enhance these text-based datasets for speech recognition, we generated corresponding audio data using the AWS Polly text-to-speech (TTS) service. To support future research on joint ASR and NER, we will release all datasets.

Baselines.  We evaluate our WhisperNER againt natural two-stage baselines, in which we initially employ a Whisper large-v2 model for speech-to-text transcription, followed by a NER model. We constrain our evaluation to recent small-scale NER models (up to 0.5 billion parameters) to ensure competitiveness and maintain parity in terms of the number of parameters and computational requirements, aligning with our end-to-end approach. The baselines include: (i) GLiNER [8]: a transformer encoder based architecture with zero-shot NER capabilities. We use the large variant, GLiNER-L; (ii) NuNER [9]: since the original NuNER model is not suitable for the zero-shot NER task, we utilize a GLiNER based architecture model, trained with the synthetic dataset of [9], named NuNER zero-span.; (iii) GNER [11]: Different from the previous two benchmarks, this approach uses generative models specifically adopted for the NER task. We use the flan-t5-base variant; (iv) WhisperNER-BIO: Our approach with complete BIO annotations; (v) WhisperNER: Our approach with NER span boundary annotations (Figure 1).

Evaluation metrics.  We assess the performance of our system using two evaluation metrics: Word Error Rate (WER), which quantifies the accuracy of the automatic speech recognition transcription, and F1 Score, which evaluates the effectiveness of named entity recognition. For a NER span prediction to be deemed correct, both the transcription must be accurate and the assigned NER label must match the true entity. This dual requirement ensures that our evaluation reflects both the precision of the speech recognition and the correctness of the entity identification. For the baseline methods, we compute the WER metric on the ASR’s output.

Training details.  The WhisperNER models are trained using a subset of 350350350K samples from the dataset proposed in [9]. For each example, we generate a corresponding synthetic audio sample. Unless stated otherwise, we train the WhisperNER models for 250250250K steps, with learning rate 1​e−61𝑒61e-6 and linear decay learning rate scheduler. During training, we freeze the Whisper encoder and only modify the decoder.

Each test example is annotated with positive NER tags based on the annotation scheme of [9]. To construct negative samples, we randomly sample negative NER labels from the entire dataset. We ensure that the number of negative NER labels matches the number of positive labels, resulting in a balanced dataset. III-D Ablation

To better understand the impact of negative sampling strategies and entity type dropout on model performance, we conducted an ablation study. We analyzed varying the proportion of negative samples, the method for negative sampling, and the inclusion of random entity type dropout during training. For efficiency and consistency, all models were trained for 25K steps using the augmented NuNER dataset [9] and evaluated on the MIT NER benchmarks.

Table IV shows that using full entity sets from negative examples, “random sample”, consistently outperforms random negative sampling of NER types, likely due to the contextual consistency of the selected examples. We also tested a “hard negative” approach, where negative examples are chosen based on semantic similarity to positive examples. While this method performed comparably, it introduced additional overhead in querying the dataset, hence we opt for random sampling from the training set for efficiency.

Figure 3 demonstrates that using two negative samples (approximately 66%percent6666\% negative entity types) provides the best F1 and WER results. Figure 2 highlights the effectiveness of random entity type dropout, which helps reducing entity tags hallucinations during inference. Based on these findings, we train WhisperNER with entity type dropout and negative sampling based on the entity set from two negative examples.

IV Conclusion

In this paper, we propose WhisperNER, a novel approach for joint open type named entity recognition and automatic speech recognition. Our model is designed to generalize to novel entities at inference, effectively handling a diverse range of entity types. We demonstrate that WhisperNER not only outperforms traditional pipeline-based baselines in terms of entity recognition accuracy but also achieves this with minimal to no increase in transcription errors. This work marks a step forward in the integration of speech and language processing, significantly improving both the effectiveness and precision of speech-driven applications.
