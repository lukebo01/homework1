We Need Variations in Speech Synthesis:
Sub-center Modelling for Speaker Embeddings

Ismail Rasim Ulgen, , Carlos Busso , John H. L. Hansen
and Berrak Sisman Manuscript submitted on 4 Jul. Abstract

In speech synthesis, modeling of rich emotions and prosodic variations present in human voice are crucial to synthesize natural speech. Although speaker embeddings have been widely used in personalized speech synthesis as conditioning inputs, they are designed to lose variation to optimize speaker recognition accuracy. Thus, they are suboptimal for speech synthesis in terms of modeling the rich variations at the output speech distribution. In this work, we propose a novel speaker embedding network which utilizes multiple class centers in the speaker classification training rather than a single class center as traditional embeddings. The proposed approach introduces variations in the speaker embedding while retaining the speaker recognition performance since model does not have to map all of the utterances of a speaker into a single class center. We apply our proposed embedding in voice conversion task and show that our method provides better naturalness and prosody in synthesized speech.

Index Terms:
speaker embedding, speech synthesis, voice conversion, intra-class variance

I Introduction

Speaker embeddings are originally designed for the task of speaker recognition, where the aim is to recognize the speakerâ€™s identity from speech. They are vectors obtained from one of the last hidden layers of deep neural networks trained to classify speakers on a relatively large amount of data with many speakers [1, 2, 3]. Since they are advanced deep neural networks trained with a large amount of data, speaker embeddings are generalizable and capable of encoding speaker information even from very limited data. These strengths make speaker embeddings a very popular tool for various speech synthesis tasks, including text-to-speech (TTS) [4, 5] and voice conversion (VC) [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11].

Zero-shot, multi-speaker speech synthesis methods [6, 4, 7, 5] are becoming increasingly popular as they are capable of synthesizing speech from any speaker with very limited data, allowing personalization and modification of synthesized speech for various use cases. There are two main zero-shot, multi-speaker speech synthesis tasks that differ in terms of their input modality: TTS and VC. In TTS, the input text is mapped into speech, while in VC, the input speech is mapped into a target speakerâ€™s voice while preserving the linguistic content. In the zero-shot, multi-speaker setting, deep speaker embeddings are widely used as conditioning inputs in the mapping in order to make the model learn the speaker identity knowledge and be able to synthesize speech from speakers that are unseen in the training. In speech synthesis tasks, the main aim is to synthesize the most natural speech, and being able to model the variations in speech (e.g., prosody, emotion, style) that change voice characteristics of a speaker, is crucial to attaining that aim.

Since speaker recognition aims to distinguish a given speakerâ€™s utterances from others, the focus is on minimizing intra-class variance and maximizing inter-class variance [12, 13]. However, minimizing intra-class variance often results in the loss of variability between utterances by the same speaker within the speaker embeddings. It is shown that traditional speaker embedding networks with classification objective provide a single class center for each class. At the last layer, the model attempts to make embedding of an utterance close to the corresponding class center [14]. We note that pushing every class member to one single center might result in losing valuable sub-class variation information such as emotional and prosodic cues which are essential for naturalistic speech synthesis. The absence of variation within speaker embeddings might lead to suboptimal speech synthesis as the synthesis method has to model the variations in the output speech distribution of a speaker with very limited knowledge at the input. â€ â€ Speech samples: https://lec-synt.github.io/sub-center-demo/

Recently, classification with sub-class centers has been introduced [14, 15] for fine-grained image retrieval where the variation and intra-class modeling is also important for well-sorted class members. In this work, we claim that a larger intra-class variance in speaker embeddings might be more suitable for synthesis tasks, as in the case of fine-grained image retrieval [16, 17]. We show that sub-centers allows introducing variation in speaker embeddings as all of utterances of a given speaker does not need to be collapsed into a single-class center.

In this work, we apply sub-class center modeling to state-of-the-art speaker embeddings for better intra-class variance modeling for zero-shot, multi-speaker speech synthesis tasks. We show that sub-center modeling allows for higher intra-class variance without compromision recognition performance; in fact, it leads to improvement. We apply our speaker embeddings in the voice conversion task, show improvement on synthesized speech. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: 1) We introduce novel speaker embeddings with subcenter modeling; 2) We provide a novel analysis of speaker embeddings in speech synthesis from the intra-class variance perspective; and 3) We show that when applied to voice conversion, the proposed embeddings with higher intra-class variance exhibit more naturalness and consistent prosody compared to embeddings with less intra-class variance.

II Related Work

Sub-center classification is relatively more popular in computer vision. Studies such as [14] and [15] have utilized sub-center modeling and improved fine-grained image retrieval. In [18], the authors applied sub-center modeling to the teacher-student modeling problem, demonstrating that the teacher model can learn sub-classes despite being trained only with parent class labels. Diverging from these approaches, the authors of [19] focused on separating the noisy labeled class examples from correctly labeled ones by using sub-centers in face recognition tasks. Instead of summing sub-center logits in the final output as previous works, they select the maximum sub-center logit and disregard the others to have highly distinct sub-centers for correct and incorrect class members.

The sub-center classification with noisy-label aspect is commonly applied in speaker recognition from unlabeled data [20, 21]. However, the commonly used sub-center modeling in image-retrieval tasks which applies summation of sub-center logits, is fundamentally different from the noisy-label aspect of sub-centers. Former type of sub-center modeling seems to be overlooked in speaker recognition approaches. In this work, we adopt sub-center modeling from fine-grained image retrieval tasks to speaker embeddings. We demonstrate its effectiveness in better modeling speaker identity in the context of multi-speaker, zero-shot speech synthesis.

III Sub-center Speaker Embeddings

In this work, we build upon one of the state-of-the-art speaker embedding methods: Emphasized Channel Attention, Propagation and Aggregation in TDNN or shortly ECAPA-TDNN [2]. ECAPA-TDNN is trained on a large amount of labeled data with speaker classification objective and has been very successful in speaker recognition. We introduce sub-class centers to ECAPA-TDNN by modifying the output layer with the objective of making it more suitable for speech synthesis.

III-A ECAPA-TDNN Speaker Embeddings

ECAPA-TDNN is a deep neural network architecture based on TDNN, which is popular in many speech applications. As the main block, the model employs squeeze-and-excitation Res2Net blocks together with TDNN layers. The model also leverages channel-dependent attentive statistical pooling to distill and focus on the important knowledge going through the deep layers. On top of stacked main blocks, the model uses temporal pooling to gather information from frame-level activations to a fixed-size vector, which is the speaker embedding. In the training phase, after temporal pooling, the speaker embedding is fed to a classifier head, and the model is trained with additive angular margin softmax loss with speaker labels. IV Voice Conversion with Speaker Embeddings

In our study, we concentrate on VC [22], which is a sub-task of speech synthesis. VC focuses on altering the speaker identity of a given speech while preserving its linguistic content. We applied our proposed speaker embeddings to a state-of-the-art (SOTA) encoder-decoder-based voice conversion method [10], where the speech is encoded to linguistic content, pitch, and speaker representations by pre-trained encoders and decoded back to speech with a Hifi-GAN vocoder [23] in the training phase. At inference, the method performs voice conversion by extracting linguistic and pitch embeddings from the source speech, while extracting speaker embeddings from a reference speech from the target speaker. The output speech is decoded as the target speakerâ€™s voice uttering the source linguistic content.

IV-A Linguistic Representations

For the linguistic representation, the VC method uses discrete HuBERT units [10, 24]. Discrete HuBERT units are obtained by applying k-means clustering to the frame-level, continuous HuBERT features. Due to the automatic speech recognition (ASR)-focused objective of masked unit prediction, linguistic information is expected to be dominant in HuBERT features. HuBERT takes raw waveform as input, and we use the 6th layer frame-level outputs from the model for clustering. We use a k-means clustering model with K=100, trained on the LibriSpeech-clean-100 [25] dataset.

IV-B Pitch Representations

The method also encodes normalized pitch contour into discrete pitch units. The pitch sequence is extracted by Dio[26] algorithm with 20 ms window and 5 ms shift. The pitch contour is normalized using the pitch statistics of the corresponding speaker.The normalized pitch contour is fed into a VQ-VAE based encoder-decoder architecture [27]. V Experimental Setup

V-A Datasets

For the speaker embedding training, we used the VoxCeleb2 dataset [29]. For voice conversion, we employed the VCTK corpus. VCTK contains a total of 110 speakers with 400 utterances from each speaker. For the VC experiments, 90 speakers are randomly chosen for training, while the remaining 20 speakers are utilized for zero-shot VC experiments as unseen speakers.

V-B Training

The baseline standard ECAPA-TDNN is trained with the implementation and recipe provided by SpeechBrain [30]. We implemented the sub-center ECAPA-TDNN on top of that implementation and trained with the default parameters. In the training, Adam optimizer is used with a base learning rate of 1e-4 and a cyclic schedule. The batch size is 32, and online augmentation (noise addition, reverberation) is applied to batch utterances as in [2]. The margin mğ‘šm and scale sğ‘ s parameters for the AAM-Softmax loss are 0.40.40.4 and 303030, respectively. For the VC training, we used the implementation in speech-resynthesis [10]. We modified the original implementation to incorporate ECAPA-TDNN embeddings as the speaker representations. All the encoders for linguistic, f0, and speaker representations are trained independently and beforehand. The HifiGAN vocoder is trained while those encoders explained in Section 3 are used as pre-trained and remain frozen. We used default parameters as in [10].

Subjective evaluation results in Table III and Fig. 2 show that embeddings with the highest intra-class variance are the best for naturalness, proving the effectiveness of introducing variation to speaker embedding. In the ABX preference test for prosody, embeddings with the highest variance outperform those with the lowest variance. The proposed sub-center modeling improves upon the baseline in almost all results. While lower intra-class variance offers better speaker discrimination than the baseline, higher variance embeddings are significantly better at capturing voice variations and synthesizing the most natural speech with the highest speaker similarity.

VII Conclusion

Originally designed for speaker recognition, speaker embeddings are widely used in synthesis tasks due to their advantages. Our work highlights the discrepancies between speaker recognition and speech synthesis, introducing speaker embeddings that are more suitable for synthesis. We propose incorporating variation information into speaker embeddings which enhances speaker discriminability at the same time. Applying our proposed embeddings to the VC task shows that those with higher intra-class variance perform best in naturalness and speaker similarity, as they better model the real speech distribution. Additionally, prosody-focused evaluations demonstrate that the proposed embeddings excel in modeling speech variations.
