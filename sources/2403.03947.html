Can Audio Reveal Music Performance Difficulty? Insights from the Piano Syllabus Dataset

Pedro Ramoneda1, Minhee Lee2, Dasaem Jeong2, J.J. Valero-Mas6, Xavier Serra1 1Corresponding author: pedro.ramoneda@upf.edu 1Music Technology Group, Universitat Pompeu Fabra, Barcelona 2MALer Lab, Sogang University, Seoul 6GRFIA, University of Alicante, Alicante
Abstract

Automatically estimating the performance difficulty of a music piece represents a key process in music education to create tailored curricula according to the individual needs of the students. Given its relevance, the Music Information Retrieval (MIR) field depicts some proof-of-concept works addressing this task that mainly focus on high-level music abstractions such as machine-readable scores or music sheet images. In this regard, the potential of directly analyzing audio recordings has been generally neglected, which prevents students from exploring diverse music pieces that may not have a formal symbolic-level transcription. This work pioneer on the automatic estimation of performance difficulty of music pieces on audio recordings with two precise contributions: (i) the first audio-based difficulty estimation dataset—namely, Piano Syllabus (PSyllabus) dataset— featuring 7,901 piano pieces across 11 difficulty levels from 1,233 composers; and (ii) a recognition framework capable of managing different input representations—both in unimodal and multimodal manners—directly derived from audio to perform the difficulty estimation task. The comprehensive experimentation comprising different pre-training schemes, input modalities, and multi-task scenarios proves the validity of the proposal and establishes PSyllabus as a reference dataset for audio-based difficulty estimation in the MIR field. The dataset as well as the developed code and trained models are publicly shared to promote further research in the field.

I Introduction

Estimating the difficulty of performing a music piece represents a key task in the music education field as it allows the adequate design of the learning curriculum of a student [1, 2]. Nevertheless, the manual assignment of the difficulty levels to music pieces is not only laborious and time-consuming, but it is also prone to errors due to the inherent subjectivity of the task [3].

This need has led to more research in this area, as shown by studies like those by Sebastien et al.[4], Chiu et al.[5], Nakamura et al.[6], and others. It has also led to the development of automatic tools for figuring out music difficulty by big music companies like Muse Group[7, 8] and Yousician [9], highlighting its importance.

Prior research on assessing the difficulty of piano pieces has predominantly concentrated on symbolic, machine-readable scores, as documented by several studies [4, 5, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 3]. Initial investigations delved into developing feature engineering descriptors [4, 5] and examining the link between piano fingering patterns and their difficulty levels [11, 12, 13]. A more recent study [14] applied stacked recurrent neural networks alongside context attention strategies for the difficulty classification of machine-readable scores. This approach utilized embeddings derived from automated piano fingering [16], expressive piano generation [17], and score details. The study highlighted the effectiveness of framing the score difficulty classification challenge as an ordinal regression issue [18] and emphasized the necessity of using complete compositions for model training to prevent reduced accuracy, rather than mere excerpts. Finally, other studies focus on estimating the performance difficulty of other instruments [19], because the difficulty is instrument dependent.

Previous research on this topic has concentrated on symbolic formats as well as sheet images, which facilitate the organization of music libraries. Nevertheless, this paper focuses on a broader approach by directly analyzing audio. This shift is critical as it enables students to explore a wider range of music through streaming platforms or to search for piano pieces they are interested in easily. This accessibility is anticipated to encourage students to actively discuss their learning preferences with their piano teachers, significantly increasing their motivation and engagement in the learning process.

One of the primary challenges in working with audio is achieving a representation that allows for direct analysis, akin to the challenges faced with sheet music and symbolic music representations. While the field of Automatic Music Transcription (AMT) has seen significant advancements for piano [10, 20], directly analyzing audio from spectral features is still done for musicological studies [21] or automated classification [22].

We propose using a straightforward architecture for summarising the performance difficulty of the musical pieces. A neural network that integrates Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), and Attention mechanisms can be described as a CNN-RNN-attention architecture. This hybrid model leverages the strengths of each component: CNNs for processing audio features, RNNs for handling sequential data, and Attention mechanisms for focusing on specific parts of the data sequence for improved performance. This architecture allows us to conduct experiments with CQT representations [23] and midi-transcribed representations [24] or combining both.

This paper also explores how to train with auxiliary tasks. We modify the baseline architecture after the attention mechanism to handle multi-task scenarios during training. After the attention mechanism, a separate fully connected block is designated for each task to learn their specific annotations. We train models on composer classification, era classification, and multiple rankings. Although the primary difficulty annotation is available for all tasks, the auxiliary tasks lack annotations for all the samples in the dataset.

Additionally, it is important to tackle the lack of data to help establish performance difficulty analysis tasks in the Music Information Retrieval community. As of now, the Mikrokosmos-difficulty (MK) [13] and Can I Play It? (CIPI) [14] symbolic datasets stand for the only available annotated collections, out of which music sheet images can be obtained by engraving mechanisms. In previous research on music sheet images [25], they use more than 7500 music pieces, openly available, in three different datasets, each one with a different difficulty grading. We propose to use a collection never used in computational research, Piano Syllabus 111Available at: https://pianosyllabus.com/. After a semi-automatic cleaning process, we propose a 7901-piece dataset with more than 1233 different composers spanning 11 difficulty labels. It is the biggest dataset with the same grading system, for piano difficulty estimation. The PSyllabus Dataset is also comparable with the biggest audio piano dataset, the GiantMIDI Piano dataset [24] which contains 10855 pieces. Finally, women compositions represent 14% of the pieces, a greater representation compared with previous datasets where these types of compositions are fully unrepresented.

Finally, we introduce two small benchmark datasets. The first follows the black women composers benchmark with 17 music pieces presented in previous research [14] aimed at assessing the generalization capabilities of difficulty models. Therefore, we have compiled 42 audio pieces by Black women composers, contained in the Hidden Voices Project [26] and categorized by difficulty.

Considering all the above, our precise contributions are:

  • •

    We adopt a CNN+RNN+Attention network as a baseline for modeling performance difficulty from multiple audio representations.

  • •

    We evaluate our proposal using a novel audio collection, the PSyllabus dataset, which is larger than previous approaches.

  • •

    We conduct extensive experiments to assess the proposed methodologies, including a zero-shot scenario for testing generalization in an out-domain distribution, multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks, or training with multiple difficulty rankings.

II The Piano Syllabus Dataset

Piano Syllabus is a web community created in 2014 by Ian Wheaton aiming the same target of this research: The repertoire of piano pieces is very extensive and there is no big syllabus with all the piano pieces ranked. Therefore, this community led by its creator has spent the last 10 years compiling its Piano Syllabus ranking containing more than 12 thousand pieces and ranging in 11 levels of difficulty. This research aims to leverage the domain knowledge kept in the Piano Syllabus to create deep learning models that continue with the same mission of this community.

The section is organized as follows: we present the methodology for creating the dataset in Subsection II-A while subsection II-B exposes the statistics about the dataset.

II-A Dataset Creation

The Piano Syllabus community contains high-quality annotations, but we encountered two main issues when creating an audio and difficulty dataset. The first issue is the absence of audio or missing YouTube links for some pieces, a common challenge also noted in the largest YouTube-related dataset, AudioSet [27], which lost over 10% of its audio since its publication [28]. The second issue involves the difficulty levels being linked to specific parts of a video, making it challenging for machine learning models due to the inconsistency in difficulty across different parts of the same video. Although the second issue is problematic from a machine learning perspective it is not from the use case of the Piano Syllabus community because humans can realize this.

To address the second issue, we developed a two-phase dataset creation method: using heuristics and an LLM validator. Initially, we collected 12,140 pieces from the Piano Syllabus Corpus, with 9,829 having both difficulty metadata and a downloadable YouTube link. We also downloaded chapters from YouTube videos, resulting in 1,754 chapters, and all the metadata associated, including eras and composer information.

Upon analyzing the 9,774 pieces, we identified noise in the corpus that could potentially decrease model performance. We manually excluded 406 pieces and established criteria for exclusion, shown in Figure 2. We identified correct matches and three error types: completely different pieces, partial matches, and overlaps with additional content.

During the heuristic phase, we refined the dataset by removing 94 piano concertos and identifying 1,658 duplicate YouTube links, indicating multiple annotations for a single video. We found 1,928 pieces marked with a start time but no end, suggesting they are part of a larger recording. By extracting and trimming audio based on YouTube chapter information, we cleaned the dataset further.

Using ChatGPT-4 API, we validated our refined sets against the criteria established, significantly reducing false positives by prompting specific instructions, as shown in Figure 3. Finally, We did a random sampling of the metadata of 100 pieces from the final dataset and 100 pieces from the pieces detected as wrong by ChatGPT. The cleaned subsets had 99 pieces matching the video and the PSyllabus metadata while the wrong detected have only 19 of 100 showing that this method has very few false positives but some false negatives.

To sum up, the final dataset has 7901 pieces. The cleaning process resulted from excluding manually 406 pieces, 55 benchmark pieces, and 94 concertos from the original 9829 pieces dataset. Consequently, we excluded 525 pieces with a prompting strategy. Finally, we discarded 848 pieces with duplicates and insufficient metadata. After this meticulous cleaning process, combining manual efforts and automated validation has resulted in a robust dataset for further research in audio, machine learning, and difficulty estimation.
