Improving Zero-Shot Chinese-English Code-Switching ASR with kğ‘˜kNN-CTC and Gated Monolingual Datastores

Abstract

The kğ‘˜kNN-CTC model has proven to be effective for monolingual automatic speech recognition (ASR). However, its direct application to multilingual scenarios like code-switching, presents challenges. Although there is potential for performance improvement, a kğ‘˜kNN-CTC model utilizing a single bilingual datastore can inadvertently introduce undesirable noise from the alternative language. To address this, we propose a novel kğ‘˜kNN-CTC-based code-switching ASR (CS-ASR) framework that employs dual monolingual datastores and a gated datastore selection mechanism to reduce noise interference. Our method selects the appropriate datastore for decoding each frame, ensuring the injection of language-specific information into the ASR process. We apply this framework to cutting-edge CTC-based models, developing an advanced CS-ASR system. Extensive experiments demonstrate the remarkable effectiveness of our gated datastore mechanism in enhancing the performance of zero-shot Chinese-English CS-ASR.

keywords:
code-switching ASR, zero-shot, kNN-CTC

1 Introduction

In today's increasingly interconnected world, the ability to accurately transcribe and understand speech in multilingual and code-switching (CS) environments is of paramount importance [1]. Automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems play a crucial role in facilitating communication across linguistic boundaries. Recent years have witnessed rapid advancements in ASR technology. However, traditional ASR models typically rely on vast amounts of labeled data for each language or dialect, posing a significant challenge in scenarios where such data is scarce or unavailable [2]. Consider, for instance, a scenario where speakers seamlessly transition between two languages, such as English and Chinese, within the same conversation. Traditional ASR systems trained on monolingual datasets struggle to accurately transcribe this CS speech, as similar pronunciations across languages could confuse the model.

Numerous studies have advanced CS-ASR. In terms of data augmentation, Chi et al. [3] introduce a method to generate CS text by forcing a multilingual machine translation system to produce CS translations. Liang et al. [4] propose a novel data augmentation method employing a text-based speech-edit model to improve CS and name entity recognition in ASR. Other studies [5, 6, 7, 8] show that jointly modeling ASR and language identity can endow models with some degree of CS capability. However, CS introduces language boundary ambiguity, which can impair the model's language recognition ability [9, 10], leading to performance degradation. To address this issue, Chen et al. [9] employ a boundary-aware predictor to acquire representations specifically designed to handle such ambiguity.

The Mixture of Experts (MoE) technique has also been employed to improve CS-ASR systems. For instance, Lu et al. [11] introduce a bi-encoder transformer network with MoE architecture to optimize data utilization. Their approach involves the separation of Chinese and English modeling using two distinct encoders to capture language-specific features effectively. Additionally, they employ a gating network to explicitly manage the language identification task. Tan et al. [12] propose a lightweight switch routing network to further refine the network. However, these methods still require fine-tuning with labeled CS data, a significant challenge due to the impracticality of covering every language pair.

Addressing this, researchers have explored zero-shot learning to enable model generalization to new tasks or languages without specific training data. Yan et al. [13] suggest a cross-lingual pseudo-labeling modification for monolingual modules to produce transliterations of foreign speech, aiming to circumvent the error propagation of frame-wise language identification (LID) decisions. Despite this advancement, their approach necessitates fine-tuning each monolingual model with cross-lingual pseudo-labeling and using dual encoders during decoding, highlighting ongoing challenges in CS-ASR development.

Recently, retrieval-augmented methods have gained significant traction in natural language processing (NLP) tasks, such as kğ‘˜kNN-LM [14] for language modeling and kğ‘˜kNN-MT [15] for machine translation, proving particularly valuable in managing low-resource scenarios. This technique has also been extensively adopted in speech processing, with numerous studies [16, 17, 18] leveraging it. Inspired by kğ‘˜kNN-LM, Zhou et al. [16] introduce kğ‘˜kNN-CTC to improve pre-trained CTC-based ASR systems [19] by integrating a kğ‘˜kNN model to retrieve CTC pseudo labels from a meticulously pruned datastore. Despite its benefits, its application is confined to monolingual settings due to reliance on a single datastore. For zero-shot CS-ASR, adopting kğ‘˜kNN-CTC with a bilingual Chinese-English datastore offers potential improvements but also poses the risk of decoding interference from extraneous language noise.

In this paper, we concentrate on zero-shot Chinese-English CS-ASR. Leveraging the kğ‘˜kNN-CTC concept, we introduce a novel approach for zero-shot Chinese-English CS-ASR. Rather than using a combined datastore of both languages, which introduces undesirable noise, we utilize two separate monolingual datastores. Our method features a gated datastore mechanism for selecting the appropriate monolingual datastore for each frame during decoding, thus ensuring the explicit injection of language-specific information.

Our main contributions are as follows:

1. We initially adapt kğ‘˜kNN-CTC for zero-shot Chinese-English CS-ASR by developing a bilingual datastore, thereby enhancing performance.

2. We then devise a kğ‘˜kNN-CTC framework that leverages two separate monolingual datastores and implements a selection mechanism to choose the appropriate datastore during decoding, ensuring the precise utilization of language-specific information in conjunction with CTC processing.

3. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach through comprehensive experimental validation.

3 Experimental setup

3.1 Dataset

We utilize ASCEND [20], a Chinese-English dataset for CS-ASR. We split the training set of ASCEND into three parts: Chinese, English, and Mixed (CS-ASR data), denoted as SCâ€‹Nsubscriptğ‘†ğ¶ğ‘S_{CN}, SEâ€‹Nsubscriptğ‘†ğ¸ğ‘S_{EN}, SMâ€‹Iâ€‹Xsubscriptğ‘†ğ‘€ğ¼ğ‘‹S_{MIX} respectively. Our experiments only employ the Chinese and English subsets for fine-tuning the models and constructing datastores to keep the zero-shot setting. Additionally, we utilize the ASCEND test set (denoted as TEST) and the mixed training set (SMâ€‹Iâ€‹Xsubscriptğ‘†ğ‘€ğ¼ğ‘‹S_{MIX}) as our test sets. The details of each subset are shown in Table 1.

3.2 Implementation details

Our experiments are conducted using the open-source toolkit WeNet [21] for Conformer [22] and HuggingFace's Transformers [23] for Wav2vec2-XLSR [24].

The Conformer-CTC baseline employs log Mel-filter banks, with a 25ms window and 10ms shift, as input. We utilize the open-source checkpoint111 https://github.com/wenet-e2e/wenet/blob/main/docs/pretrained_models.en.md pre-trained by WenetSpeech [25], comprising 12 blocks of Conformer encoder layers with 8 attention heads and 3 blocks of bi-transformer decoder layers. During finetuning, a learning rate of 5Ã—10âˆ’55superscript1055\times 10^{-5} and a batch size of 16 with 5000 warmup steps on 4 GTX 3090 GPUs are employed, with weights of 0.3 assigned to the CTC loss and 0.7 to the attention loss.

In our experimentation with the Wav2vec2-XLSR-CTC baseline, we leverage the open-source checkpoint222https://huggingface.co/jonatasgrosman/wav2vec2-large-xlsr-53-chinese-zh-cn and fine-tune the baseline with codebase333https://github.com/HLTCHKUST/ASCEND provided by ASCEND [20]. We abbreviate Conformer-CTC to Conformer and Wav2vec2-XLSR-CTC to Wav2vec2-XLSR for simplicity. All reported results are derived using CTC greedy search decoding. It is important to note that our models are fine-tuned exclusively on the subsets SCâ€‹Nsubscriptğ‘†ğ¶ğ‘S_{CN} and SEâ€‹Nsubscriptğ‘†ğ¸ğ‘S_{EN}, thus preserving the zero-shot CS-ASR setting.

We reimplement the version of kğ‘˜kNN-CTC (full) using FAISS [26], which has demonstrated superior performance in in-domain ASR tasks [16]. For Conformer, we follow kğ‘˜kNN-CTC to determine the location of keys by extracting the output of the final encoder layer's feedforward network (FFN). For Wav2vec2-XLSR, we select the output of the Wav2vec2-XLSR encoder as the location of keys. We set kğ‘˜k to 1024 following kğ‘˜kNN-CTC for both baselines.

Regarding the gated monolingual datastore selection mechanism, we set nğ‘›n=300 for the Conformer baseline and nğ‘›n=10 for the Wav2vec2-XLSR baseline to compute the average distances of the nğ‘›n nearest neighbors from DCâ€‹Nsubscriptğ·ğ¶ğ‘D_{CN} and DEâ€‹Nsubscriptğ·ğ¸ğ‘D_{EN}. We set the distribution calibration temperature tğ‘¡t to 5 for the Conformer baseline and 200 for the Wav2vec2-XLSR baseline, adjusting the probability distribution for the alternative language.

We adopt the Mixture Error Rate (MER) as outlined in [27] for metrics. MER accounts for both Chinese characters and English words when calculating the edit distance. Errors are tallied separately for Chinese and English based on the language of the reference token.

4 Results

4.1 Zero-shot CS-ASR results

The evaluation results for the Conformer and Wav2vec2-XLSR baselines are summarized in Table 2 and Table 3, respectively. Our approach, employing the kğ‘˜kNN-CTC with a single datastore DAâ€‹Lâ€‹Lsubscriptğ·ğ´ğ¿ğ¿D_{ALL}, as well as our method utilizing dual monolingual datastores DCâ€‹Nsubscriptğ·ğ¶ğ‘D_{CN} and DEâ€‹Nsubscriptğ·ğ¸ğ‘D_{EN}, outperform the fine-tuned CTC method across both baseline models and test sets. Moreover, our integrated method, incorporating dual monolingual datastores with a gated datastore selection mechanism, demonstrates superior performance in all scenarios. This highlights that the utilization of the bilingual datastore introduces distracting noise during the retrieval process, which adversely affects performance. By implementing the gated mechanism to select a monolingual datastore, we effectively mitigate interference from alternate languages, resulting in precise retrieval and improved performance. Our approach achieves a relative MER reduction of 4.4% and 7.4% on the Tâ€‹Eâ€‹Sâ€‹Tğ‘‡ğ¸ğ‘†ğ‘‡TEST and SMâ€‹Iâ€‹Xsubscriptğ‘†ğ‘€ğ¼ğ‘‹S_{MIX} sets respectively for the Conformer baseline. Furthermore, for the Wav2vec2-XLSR baseline, we observe a relative MER reduction of 3.6% and 5.1% on the two test sets separately.

Additionally, we compute the Real Time Factor444https://openvoice-tech.net/index.php/Real-time-factor (RTF) for both the Conformer and Wav2vec2-XLSR baselines. Compared with CTC and kğ‘˜kNN, a slight increase in RTF is evident due to the kğ‘˜kNN retrieval process. This marginal slowdown is expected and deemed acceptable given the dual retrieval processes involved in our method. These results underscore the effectiveness of our proposed approach.

Specifically, we conduct ablations on the utilization of a single datastore, two separate datastores, average nğ‘›n neighbor distances for selecting datastore, and scale temperature tğ‘¡t to adjust the alternate language distribution. Employing the gated mechanism with nğ‘›n neighbors' average distances and scale temperature tğ‘¡t, we achieve the best performance on both datasets.

5 Conclusion

In this paper, we propose a kğ‘˜kNN-CTC framework that utilizes dual monolingual datastores and implements a gated datastore selection mechanism for zero-shot Chinese-English CS-ASR. Compared to using a bilingual datastore, our method avoids undesirable noise from the alternate language and facilitates the selection of the appropriate monolingual datastore for each frame during decoding. This ensures the explicit injection of language-specific information. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach for zero-shot Chinese-English CS-ASR.
